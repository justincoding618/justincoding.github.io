FBS Playoff Reform

College football has been a spectacle where fans are passionate about their teams. However, many debated how current and past postseason systems have come up short of how national champions are determined. The controversies grew louder in the 1990s. The BCS finally pitted the top two teams from 1998 to 2013. Then, the College Football Playoff took over since 2014. As at least some already know, it started as a 4-team field and lasted for ten seasons. The playoff would then expand to a 12-team field since 2024.

The FBS Playoff Reform project roots back to my childhood days in the 2000s. My parents engrained me as an Ohio State fan growing up. I became part of the Ohio State fandom in 2006 when Ohio State was able to beat Michigan in an instant Game of the Century classic. Since then, I became interested in how college football determined their national champion. As the administered systems did not do well in determining teams to play for a National Championship, that was when I wanted to figure out how I could develop a system that would best determine teams who would play for a National Championship.

When the 4-team College Football Playoff was approved back in 2012, I was excited, but skeptical. During my college years, I always wondered whether 4 teams was enough to determine a National Champion. When the system first debuted in 2014, it already showed flaws. Even with the expansion to 12 teams in 2024, I was not impressed with the CFP system and the people who were in charge of the system. After graduating from the University of Missouri (aka Mizzou), I started creating my own project on September 10, 2016.

At first, I made a multi-hundred page word document about improving the college football postseason and the adjustments needed to make it happen. However, as years have gone, I started to realize that trying to keep up with the document by myself wasn't going to help me get anywhere. I needed to do something where I could showcase over a decade of work.

You're likely asking me, "Have you really worked on this project for a very long time?" If you have asked this question, you are not alone. To answer the question, yes, it is true. I have been working on my college football project since September 10, 2016. I have still kept going.

After a little over nine years of trying to keep up with proposing a new playoff system on my multi-page word document, I decided to put my developer knowledge into good hands. I applied my knowledge of HTML, CSS, and JavaScript and made the conscious effort to put the work that I have done onto a website. I have deployed the website through GitHub pages on February 9, 2026.

Since deploying the website, I have made conscientious efforts to keep improving. The website has over 60 HTML pages, with four reusable HTML elements, 4 CSS files, and 5 JS files. Out of the five JS files, four of them are custom made by me, and one is reused from JQuery (specifically, the jquery.min.js file). The website is deployed on GitHub pages. However, in the long-term, I hope to provide my own backend for my website so that I can take charge of my own website.

The FBS Playoff reform project is a project that I have done independently. Without the conscientious effort to make the website, nearly no one would be able to understand how much work I have put into my project. For those who can truly understand how much time and effort I have invested to make a playoff project that would benefit the entire Division I FBS ecosystem, it really means a lot to me. I sincerely hope my project has a lot of meaning to you too.
